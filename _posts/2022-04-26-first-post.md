As I am building this website, I thought that a good first post would be a brief description of what my PhD is about. As happens with most students, my research questions and topic have mutated since my first days, and continue to do so, which is why this will only be an accurate description for +/- 6 months.

My project is funded by a Scientia scholarship called "Warm water intrusions and ice melt at the Antarctic margin". However, that never really described my project because when I told my supervisor I was interested in large scale ocean dynamics, he suggested I focus on the Ross and Weddell Gyres, the two polar gyres of the Southern Ocean. My tool of preference would be [ACCESS-OM2](http://cosima.org.au/index.php/models/access-om2/) numerical model.

However, little is known about the large scale dynamics of this gyres, including their variability in connection to possible forcing mechanisms. And so the task became enormous and suddenly two gyres seemed too much for me to handle.. and I chose the Weddell. For many reasons, which I won't go into detail here.

So far, it has been a lot of fun. Completed my [first project](https://julia-neme.github.io/portfolio/jgr-21) and got a bit distracted after that thanks to a colaborative project delving into the [sensitivity of the Antarctic margin to the easterly winds](https://github.com/adele157/easterlies-collaborative-project). This project proved to be even more fun, even more so its horizontal working dynamics. All of our conversations and work-in-progress goes through a github repo, making the science behind transparent for everyone involved.

Part of the colaboration involved a small, tangential work looking at future projections of the easterly winds in CMIP6 models, which I carried out on my own and is at the moment being reviewed (but available as a [preprint](https://www.essoar.org/doi/10.1002/essoar.10510952.1)).

But I'm just past my 2 year mark, looking forward to 2 more years of research into the Weddell Gyre!
