There are a large number of oceanic processes that depend upon and are influenced by surface winds around the Antarctic margin, amongst which are bottom water production, cross-shelf transport and sea ice formation and drift. These processes, mostly hapenning over a narrow continental shelf, have the potential of influencing the global climate and play a dominant role in our planet's response to climate change. 

For example, in a handfull of locations around the Antarctic margin we find the specific conditions for the production of dense waters, a process that extracts carbon from the atmosphere to subsequently store it in the abyssal ocean. Dense waters formed at the Antarctic margin constitute the bulk of the global abyssal ocean, and there is evidence that the rates of formation and export of these waters are sensitive to surface winds. 

However, there is little to no research devoted to understanding projected changes in the Antarctic surface winds, usually referred to as "easterlies". In the context of a larger project that explores the sensitivity of the Antarctic margin to the easterlies, I analysed CMIP6[^1] models to quantify future changes until the end of the 21st Century. Here's the [full article](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359562187_Projected_changes_of_surface_winds_over_the_Antarctic_continental_margin), currently under reviewed. But I'll spoil my main takeaway below:

The figure shows how in high and moderate emission scenarios, CMIP6 models show a weakening of surface winds, both in the south-to-north and east-to-west directions. The weakening becomes more pronounced for the high emission scenario, amounting to 23% and 7% respectively for both components. This weakening is bound to have an impact on a variety of ocean processes, and calls for more research, as well as improvements in how models re-solve atmospheric and oceanic processes in high latitudes.
