## Weddell Gyre: seasonal and interannual variability in connection with possible forcing mechanisms
### Co-authors: Matthew England, Andrew McC. Hogg

This work is published and accessible through [JGR: Oceans](https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2021JC017662)

The Weddell Gyre is one of the major features of the circulation south of the Antarctic Circumpolar Current (ACC) and as such it mediates how relatively warm waters from the ACC are able to reach the Antarctic margin. The gyre becomes almost completely covered by sea ice during part of the year which hampers long-term, continuous observations. Its mean transport has been reported to be anything in between 30 and 100 Sv, a wide range attributed to the fact that this measure is very dependent on the methodology used. For example, whether its based on summer-time hydrographic observations or a numerical model.

<img align="right" width="60%" src="/assets/img/portfolio/wg-schematic.png">


There are several processes that make the region relevant for the global climate. The Filchner Ronne Ice Shelf, in the north-western corner of the region, is one of the four locations of dense water production around Antarctica. In fact, around 40% of bottom waters in the global ocean are coming from the Weddell and its particular properties have been suggested to depend on the gyre’s circulation. One of the most important source water maters for their production is Circumpolar Deep Water (CDW) that is advected by the gyre, undergoing mixing and upwelling in its path until reaching the ice shelf. CDW is able to enter the region because the gyre has an open eastern boundary, which is another feature that makes the Weddell different from its subtropical counterparts. 

However the region is under sampled, with a large summer-time bias in observations which means that we don’t know much about the gyre’s variability across different time scales, and less even about the driving mechanisms of this variability, which are all important questions looking forward regarding the Antarctic’s response to climate change. So these are our research questions in this work. We used a global, ocean sea ice coupled numerical model called ACCESS-OM2, that at its highest resolution configuration was able to adequately resolve observed features of the region. Using a numerical model means that we were able to work with a complete, 61-year long dataset to characterise the Weddell Gyre's variability.

We found that the gyre's mean transport is 41 Sv, which fits well within past estimates, and as you can see in the barotropic stream function figure next, it’s got a double lobed circulation structure, as well as an eddy rich eastern boundary, manifested in this decay of the streamfunction in the region. Both of these features have appeared in observations.

<img align="right" width="60%" src="/assets/img/portfolio/figure_6_e.jpg">


Regarding the gyre's variability, we found a large seasonal cycle with a winter intensification of around 10 Sv, and a summer weakening of similar magnitude. Note that this means that summer-time based estimates are not going to be representative of the actual mean transport. 

We also found significant interannual variability, as you can see in the time series of anomalies with respect to the seasonal cycle next, with years of anomalously strong and weak circulation that spun up or down the gyre in around 10% (and this is 25% with respect to the gyre's mean transport). However, we didn’t find significant correlations with any of our proposed forcing mechanisms, which were: surface stress curl, surface buoyance fluxes and the Southern Annular Mode. Note here, that unlike other studies, we used surface stress curl taking into account the presence of sea ice that acts to dampen the transfer of momentum. For convenience, most past studies use wind as the sole influence on surface stress. We did find a weak, significant correlation with the strength of the easterly winds at the southern boundary of the gyre. 

<img align="right" width="60%" src="/assets/img/portfolio/wg-timeseries.png">

The benefit of using a numerical model, is that now we have a 61-year long time series to play around with. What we chose to do, was a composite analysis of strong and weak events to see how the gyre influences the region. Amongst others, we found changes in subsurface temperature maximum, which indicates changes in the inflow of CDW into the region; and large changes in sea ice concentration, with an increase of around 10% along the northern rim of the gyre during strong years. The hatching indicates where these variables correlate with our time series here, which means for example, that we can expect sea ice at the north to depend on the gyre’s strength.
